
# Instructions for progress reports


This document explains what you need to submit, when, and how, for each progress update during the hackathon.

---

## What you are submitting

A short progress report answering three questions, plus one visual artifact. The report is not a formal deliverable — it is a snapshot of where your project stands. Be concrete and honest: a sharp description of a negative result is more useful than vague claims of progress.

---

## The three questions

**Q1 — What have you built or discovered so far?**
Describe concretely what you have implemented, computed, or found. Mention specific tools, models, or datasets you have used or processed. Avoid vague claims — if you ran an experiment, state what it showed.
*(~150–250 words)*

**Q2 — What is your core claim or insight?**
State the most interesting or surprising result, pattern, or idea your project has produced so far. This could be a quantitative finding, a qualitative observation, an unexpected failure that changed your direction, or a sharp hypothesis you are now testing. If you have no result yet, state the most concrete thing you have learned.
*(~100–200 words)*

**Q3 — What is your plan for the next phase, and what is your biggest open risk?**
Describe what you will do next and what could go wrong or block you. Be specific about the risk — "we don't know if X will work because Y" is more useful than "we hope to finish on time."
*(~100–150 words)*

---

## The visual artifact

**Required. Choose exactly one of the following:**

- A figure (chart, plot, embedding projection, etc.)
- A table (results, data quality summary, examples with labels, etc.)
- A screenshot of your dashboard, showing what it displays and what it allows a user to do

The artifact must directly illustrate something stated in your answers above — not a placeholder, not a pipeline diagram, not a mockup of something you plan to build.

Add a caption of 1–2 sentences explaining what it shows and why it matters.

## What makes a good report

The goal of these updates is not to impress — it is to help you think clearly about your project and to let us give you useful feedback. The most common mistakes are:

- Describing what you *plan* to do instead of what you *have* done
- Stating an insight that is actually just a restatement of the project premise (e.g. "ESG reporting has increased over time" is a motivation, not a finding)
- Identifying a risk as "we might not have enough time" — this is always true and tells us nothing; name the specific technical or methodological uncertainty
- Submitting a visual that is decorative rather than evidential (e.g. a word cloud that confirms the corpus contains ESG words)

A report where you say "we tried X, it did not work because Y, so we are now doing Z" is excellent. That is exactly the kind of information that is useful.
